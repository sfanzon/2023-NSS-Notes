8  Series

A series is the sum of all terms in a sequence: \[ a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \ldots + a_n + \ldots \] Since we are dealing with an infinite amount of terms, we need to be careful. For example, consider the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^k = -1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - \ldots \tag{8.1}\] If we sum the terms in pairs, we obtain \[ \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^k = (-1 + 1) + (- 1 + 1) + ( - 1 + 1) + \ldots = 0\,. \] If we reorder the terms, we obtain a different result \[\begin{align*} \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^k & = (1 + 1) - 1 + (1 + 1) - 1 + \ldots \\ & = (2 - 1) + (2 - 1) + \ldots \\ & = 1 + 1 + \ldots \\ & = \infty \,. \end{align*}\] Therefore commutativity of the sum does not hold when summing infinitely many terms. We need a good definition of convergence.

8.1 Convergent series

We will develop the theory of series in for sequences in \(\mathbb{C}\). All the results for complex series will also hold for series in \(\mathbb{R}\). Some results will only hold for series in \(\mathbb{R}\): this will be the case for comparison tests in which the order relation of \(\mathbb{R}\) is needed.

We start by defining partial sums.

Definition 1: Partial sums
Let \(\left(a_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{C}\). The \(n\)-th partial sum of \((a_n)\) is \[ s_{n} :=a_{1}+a_{2}+\ldots+a_{n} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k} \] This sequence \(\left(s_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) is called the sequence of partial sums.

We can use the sequence of partial sums to define convergence of a series.

Definition 2: Convergent series
Let \(\left(a_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{C}\). We denote the series of \(\left(a_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) by \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{k} \] We say that this series converges to \(s \in \mathbb{C}\) if \[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k} = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n} = s \,. \] In this case we write \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{k}=s \,. \]

Definition 3: Divergent series
Let \(\left(a_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{C}\). The series \[ \sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k \] is divergent if the sequence of partial sums \((s_n)\) is divergent.

Example 4
Consider the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k+1)} \,. \] Note that \[ \frac{1}{k(k+1)}=\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+1} \,. \] Hence, we can compute the partial sums \(s_{n}\) as follows: \[\begin{align*} s_{n} & =\sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{k(k+1)} \\ & =\sum_{k=1}^{n}\left(\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+1}\right) \\ & =\frac{1}{1}-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{4}+\ldots+\frac{1}{n}-\frac{1}{n+1} \\ & =1-\frac{1}{n+1} . \end{align*}\] Since, \[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty}\left(1-\frac{1}{n+1}\right)=1 \,, \] the series converges to 1 , i.e., \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k+1)}=1 \,. \] A series of this kind is called a telescopic sum, since we can fold the entire partial sum together, in such a way that only two terms remain.

Let us go back to the series considered in (8.1).

Example 5
Consider the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(-1)^{k} \,. \] The partial sums \(s_{n}\) are given by \[ s_{n}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}(-1)^{k}= \begin{cases}-1 & \text { if } \, n \, \text { is odd } \, \\ 0 & \text { if } \, n \, \text { is even. }\end{cases} \] Therefore \(s_{n}\) diverges, so also the series diverges.

In general, it is a difficult taks to compute the exact value of a series. Therefore, we will mainly focus our effort on determining whether a series converges or not.

The following theorem shows that if the terms in the sequence do not converge to \(0\), then the series cannot converge.

Theorem 6
Let \(\left(a_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{C}\). If the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{k} \] converges, then \[ \lim_{k \rightarrow \infty} a_{k}=0 \,. \]

Proof
Suppose that \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{k} \] converges. By definition of convergent series there exists some \(s \in \mathbb{C}\) such that \[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k}=s \,. \] Note that then also \[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n+1}=s \,. \] Hence, by the Algebra of Limits in \(\mathbb{C}\), we have that \[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}\left(s_{n+1}-s_{n}\right)=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n+1}-\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n}=s-s=0 \,. \] Noting that \[ s_{n+1}-s_{n}=a_{n+1}\,, \] we obtain \[ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} a_{n}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} a_{n+1}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty}\left(s_{n+1}-s_{n}\right)=0 \, . \]

The contrapositive statement of Theorem 6 is particularly useful. For clarity, we give the precise statement.

Theorem 7
Let \(\left(a_{n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{C}\). If \[ \lim_{k \rightarrow \infty} a_{k} \neq 0 \,, \] then the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{k} \] does not converge.

Example 8
Consider the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^k \,. \tag{8.2}\] We have that \[ \lim_{k \to \infty} a_k = \lim_{k \to \infty} (-1)^k \neq 0 \,, \] being \(a_k\) divergent. Therefore the series at (8.2) diverges by Theorem 7.

Example 9
Consider the series \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n}{5 n+11} \,. \] Then \[ a_{n}:=\frac{n}{5 n+11}=\frac{1}{5 + \dfrac{11}{n} } \longrightarrow \frac{1}{5} \neq 0 \text {. } \] Hence, the series does not converge.

Important
Theorem 7 says that if \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) converges, then \[ a_k \to 0 \,. \] In general the condition \(a_k \to 0\) does not guarantee convergence of the associated series, as shown in the example below.

Example 10

Consider the series \[ \sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k \,, \quad a_k := \frac{1}{ \sqrt{k+1} + \sqrt{k} } \,. \] By the Algebra of Limits we have \[ \lim_{k \to \infty} a_k = 0 \,. \] However the partial sums are given by \[ s_n = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k = \sqrt{n+1} - 1 \,. \] Therefore \(s_n\) is divergent and so the series is divergent.

Exercise: Prove by induction that \(\sum_{k=1}^n a_k = \sqrt{n+1} - 1\).

Remark 11
It is customary to sum a series starting at \(k=1\). However one could start the sum from any \(k=N\) with \(N \in \mathbb{N}\). This would not affect the convergence of the series, but of course it will change the value of the limit.

Example 12
We have seen that \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k+1)} = 1 \,. \] Hence also the series \[ \sum_{k=7}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k+1)} \] converges. However, in this case, the partial sums are given by \[\begin{align*} s_{n} & =\sum_{k=7}^{n} \frac{1}{k(k+1)} \\ & =\sum_{k=7}^{n}\left(\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+1}\right) \\ & =\frac{1}{7}-\frac{1}{8}+\frac{1}{8}-\frac{1}{9}+\ldots+\frac{1}{n}-\frac{1}{n+1} \\ & =\frac{1}{7}-\frac{1}{n+1} \,. \end{align*}\] Therefore \[ \sum_{k=7}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k+1)}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} s_{n}=\frac{1}{7} \,. \]
